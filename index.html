Four waves

A chronology of change

Each wave a different kind of change. They do not replace one another. They braid.

1995 – 2004

Technology

Placeholder sketch · Napkin-Solo version to come

The first decade was planned change. Global ERP and supply-chain programs: the kind of change an organization chooses, budgets, and announces. Positive ambition, clear blueprints, and still hard. No plan survives ten countries intact. What makes planned change work is the discipline of rebuilding alignment country by country, go-live by go-live.

At Intelligroup I grew from consultant to program lead, implementing ERP and digital platforms across 10+ global companies. I earned a reputation for salvaging programs that were delayed, over budget, or organizationally misaligned.

2004 – 2011

Transactions

Placeholder sketch · Napkin-Solo version to come

The second decade was unplanned change. Deals arrive suddenly, carry political weight, and compress critical decisions into weeks, often with significant change in personnel. Change without consensus, at speed. Judgment is the scarce resource.

As a founding member of EY's strategy and transaction practice, and later at Deloitte, I advised on ~25 global transactions across the deal cycle: strategy, integration, carve-out, synergy realization. Italy to China to Japan.

2011 – 2025

Transformation

Placeholder sketch · Napkin-Solo version to come

The third wave was transformation. Change thrust on companies by market and technology dynamics, and taken on willingly. Underperformers deliberately becoming different. Every company navigating digital, DTC, and omnichannel. A massive repooling of operating models and of people.

At BCG I helped build the US Fashion & Apparel sector: $1B+ in operating margin gains across five global brands on a $15B+ revenue base, a −20% quarterly revenue trajectory reversed to −3%, a wholesale business pivoted to $1B of US DTC. Then ERM, Alvarez & Marsal, and an interim CDIO chair at Columbia Sportswear.

2026 –

AI-led Transformative Force

Placeholder sketch · Napkin-Solo version to come

The fourth wave is just beginning. Very rapid, technology-led transformation in which deep-seated companies can be unsettled by nimble small players, and business models are repriced at the same time as the technology. The most complex change environment of the four. And it is additive: every prior wave is still in the braid.

My answer is to learn it the way I have led it: by doing. A structured practice across the whole stack (Work with AI), and live engines built with my own hands (Playground).

Work with AI

Own the whole stack, from the token to the boardroom.

The bar A capability counts only when I can build it and explain it from first principles.

AI is a once-in-a-generation change in how organizations work, and I refuse to understand it secondhand. So I am learning the whole stack the way I have always learned: by building. Below is the map I hold myself to, with a sample of the work and what it taught me.

Core capabilities

Six areas, learned by building

1 · The Machine

How the model works inside: attention, sampling, training, embeddings, tokens.

BuiltRan open vision models on my own hardware to find where they break. Benchmarked five models on one task, fairly.
LearnedMost failures I blamed on the model were in how I ran it.

2 · Building on the Model

Getting reliable answers out: prompts, structured output, orchestration, retrieval.

BuiltThe antilibrary engine: five specialized agents in a line, each with a versioned, tested prompt.
LearnedA prompt is a contract, not a string. Version it and test it.

3 · The Harness

Running the system in production: cost, caching, tracing, failure modes.

BuiltRebuilt an engine backend around caching and token discipline. One render got nine times cheaper.
LearnedCost lives in the context window, not on the price sheet.

4 · Evaluation & Trust

Evals, grounding, and what makes a system worth believing.

BuiltA grounding check that runs at every step, so agents check agents.
LearnedFabrication is the trust metric. The guarantees live in the harness, not the model.

5 · Product & Human Interface

Where the model meets the person.

BuiltThree products anyone can open in a browser today.
LearnedThe interface earns the trust. The person keeps the judgment.

6 · The Boardroom

Value, strategy, and governance: what this wave does to an operating model.

BuiltA capability map that translates all of the above for an executive audience.
LearnedThe constraint is the organization, not the model.
Principles

How I build

Four rules run through everything I build. Many specialized agents in a line, not one prompt doing everything. Every claim checked against its evidence. Systems that run on their own clock. And a person deciding at the step that matters.

Playground

You cannot claim to know AI if you have not built with it. Everything here runs live.

(anti)library

Sanskrit · मन्थन manthan · to churn.
The cosmic ocean, churned until treasures rise.

Open the engine ↗

A library is a self-portrait. The engine reads it.

Photograph your shelves. The engine catalogues every book, breaks each into its atomic ideas, and finds the threads running across the whole collection: the portrait you could not see from inside the frame.

MethodCollect · Classify · Churn · Curate · Consume
LearnedAsked what matters, a model gives the canonical answer. Discovery has to be architected against its own bias.
StatusLive — anti-library.ai

Napkin-Solo

English · compound.
A napkin sketch, drawn alone.

Open the engine ↗

The hardest part of visual thinking is not the drawing. It is knowing what to draw.

Feed it any text: a dense paper, a strategy brief, a tangled idea. It extracts the one insight worth illustrating, then draws the napkin sketch that makes it land. The sketches across this site are its territory.

LearnedThe engine executes. It does not decide what matters. Choosing what to draw is the product.
StatusLive — napkinsolo.ai

Napkin Dōjō

Japanese · 道場 dōjō.
The place where you return, repeat, and the skill becomes yours.

Open the engine ↗

We read a lot and remember little.

You finish a book, highlight an article, save a thread. A week later it is gone. The Dōjō brings the best ideas back, again and again, until knowing about them becomes knowing them.

LearnedSkimming feels like learning. Only returning makes it stick.
StatusLive on the web — napkinsolo.ai/dojo
