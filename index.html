Four waves

A chronology of change

Each wave a different kind of change. They do not replace one another — they braid.

1995 – 2004

Technology

Napkin-Solo sketch
Era 1 · planned change
— prompt in chat —

The first decade was planned change. Global ERP and supply-chain programs — the kind of change an organization chooses, budgets, and announces. Positive ambition, clear blueprints — and still hard. The plan survives nowhere intact; what makes planned change work is the discipline of rebuilding alignment country by country, go-live by go-live.

At Intelligroup, from consultant to program lead: ERP and digital platforms implemented across 10+ global companies — and a reputation for salvaging programs that were delayed, over budget, or organizationally misaligned.

2004 – 2011

Transactions

Napkin-Solo sketch
Era 2 · unplanned change
— prompt in chat —

The second decade was unplanned change. Deals arrive suddenly, carry political weight, and compress critical decisions into weeks — often with significant change in personnel. Change without consensus, at speed, where judgment is the scarce resource.

A founding member of EY's strategy and transaction practice, later at Deloitte: ~25 global transactions across the deal cycle — strategy, integration, carve-out, synergy realization — from Italy to China to Japan.

2011 – 2025

Transformation

Napkin-Solo sketch
Era 3 · chosen change
— prompt in chat —

The third wave was transformation. Change thrust on companies by market and technology dynamics — and taken on willingly. Underperformers deliberately becoming different; every company navigating digital, DTC, and omnichannel; a massive repooling of operating models and of people.

At BCG, helped build the US Fashion & Apparel sector: $1B+ in operating margin gains across five global brands on a $15B+ revenue base, a −20% quarterly revenue trajectory reversed to −3%, a wholesale business pivoted to $1B of US DTC. Then ERM, Alvarez & Marsal — and an interim CDIO chair at Columbia Sportswear.

2026 –

AI-led Transformative Force

Napkin-Solo sketch
Era 4 · the braid
— prompt in chat —

The fourth wave is just beginning. Very rapid, technology-led transformation in which deep-seated companies can be unsettled by nimble small players — and business models are repriced at the same time as the technology. The most complex change environment of the four, and it is additive: every prior wave is still in the braid.

The answer is to learn it the way it has always been led — by doing. A structured practice across the whole stack (Work with AI), and live engines built by hand (Playground).

Work with AI

Own the whole stack — from the token to the boardroom.

The bar A capability counts only when he can build it — and explain it from first principles well enough that people in his field learn it from him.

AI is a once-in-a-century force of change — and it cannot be understood from the executive floor alone. This is a structured learning practice across the whole stack: every capability learned by building real things, every piece of work dated and filed under the shelf it advances.

Napkin-Solo

English · compound.
A napkin sketch, drawn alone.

The hardest part of visual thinking is not the drawing. It is knowing what to draw.

Feed it any text — a dense paper, a strategy brief, a tangled idea — and it extracts the one insight worth illustrating, then draws the napkin sketch that makes it land. The sketches through this site are its work.
